Minutes of the Second Annual Proteomics Standards Initiative Spring Workshop
Siena, Italy 17-20th April 2005

Introduction

The Second Annual Spring Workshop of the Proteomics Standards Initiative (PSI) was jointly hosted by the University of Siena and the Italian Human Proteomics Organisation (HUPO). The meeting was opened by Henning Hermjakob (EMBL-EBI), who reviewed the activities to date of HUPO and in particular of the Bioinformatics Committee of which the PSI is the major activity. The Level 2.0 Molecular Interaction standards (PSI-MI) has been published in beta format, and an increasing number of databases are now making their data available in this format. MINT already release their data in both PSI-MI Level 1 and 2(beta), IntAct will do so from May onwards, DIP from June/July and Mpact (MIPS). BIND make their data available in PSI-MI Level 2(beta). Communication between these groups has been so successful that it has lead to the formation of the IMEx (International Molecular Interaction Exchange) consortium which will eventually exchange curated molecular interaction data following the nucleotide database consortium model. The work of the PSI-MI group would now concentrate on refining the current schema to allow more complete data exchange, extend the current controlled vocabularies where needed and look at developing further tools such as an XML validator and a PSI Common Query Interface (PSIQIC).

Chris Taylor (EMBL-EBI) then outlined the overall concept of the General Proteomics Standard (GPS) which will enable the increased systematic capture of all aspects of proteomics data. The already existing standards for Mass Spectrometry and Molecular Interactions (mzData and mzIdent) will exist as components within this model and other modules are currently in preparation. It is envisaged that the PSI-GPS model will be integrated with the MGED model in areas of overlap, such as sample preparation, to produce the Functional Genomics Experimental Model (FuGE) which will allow of full description of experiments where parallel samples are analysed by both microarray and proteomics techniques. Discussion with the NIH has identified the common representation of PTMs as a need by all proteomics groups, and discussion of this subject was to be an additional track for this meeting. The GPS standards will allow common representation of both methods and data but are not to be quality guidelines, although how data quality could be best represented within these models was to be a subject for discussion. MzData (Version 1.05) is now in a stable release structure and has received strong vendor support, mzIdent should be in a mature form by the HUPO congress in 2005. Work was to begin at this workshop to develop standards for 2D gel electrophoresis.

The MIAPE documentation is proceeding with a series of technology specific guidelines outlining the detail required to describe a proteomics experiment in sufficient detail to ensure full understanding of the experimental protocols utilised. The Mass Spectrometry document has been through expert review and is approaching release, the Proteoinformatics for Mass Spectrometry documentation is available in draft format. An abstract GPS object model has also been prepared (PSI-OM) and once mature, this will be used to derive an XML data exchange schema. Common ontologies for all aspects of this work are under development, although there is still some debate as to whether these should be fully developed ontologies using formats such as OWL or simpler Controlled Vocabularies (CV) as have already been designed and implemented by the PSI-MI group (Luisa Montecchi-Palazzi, EBI).

The sessions separated into three major tracks; GPS, Molecular Interactions and Mass Spectrometry.


Molecular Interactions

The session was chaired by Henning Hermjakob who summarised the advances made by the group over the last few months. A series of presentations then followed, in which a number of databases outlined both their experience in implementing PSI-MI Level 1.0 (Matthias Oesterheld Mpact (MIPS), Peter Woollard GSK, Arnaud Coel MINT, Samuel Kerriens IntAct) and the conversion from PSI-MI 1.0 to 2.0beta. Requirements for changes to the PSI-MI XML format which needed to be decided upon during his meeting were reviewed, requests have been collected all year using the SourceForge site. The aim of the meeting was to bring the PSI-MI Level 2 schema to an appropriate state of maturity for publication and full release (Level 2.5).

The addition of further CVs allowed an overall simplification of the XML schema, with attributes which were previously separately listed, for example “Protein Interactor”, “DNA Inteactor” now being described by a single field “Interactor” filled from a CV “Interactor Type”. Further typing of aliases allows full description of the molecule, whilst extension of the ExperimentalForm” of the molecule means that the exact state of the molecule used in a specific experiment, for example deletion mutants or tagged molecules, can be identified and linked to that experiment. This also allows the exchange of more complex data sets, such as a modelled interaction which can be directly linked to the experimental evidence from which it was derived.

The need to be able to fully describe complexes was identified, including the hierarchical build up of the final form from any number of sub-complexes. This requires adding the concept of “unknown” participant since the components may only be partially identified e.g. five proteins separated on a gel but only two identified by subsequent Western blot. The ability of an interaction to be used as an interactor “Complex” is now recognised which will refer back to either an internal interaction reference or a cross-reference to an eternal database.

In total, the addition of 9 further CVs was decided upon, and changes to those already existing were agreed upon Curator requests are made upon the SourceForge tracker system and discussed by an editorial board, as agreed at the Spring 2003 workshop. The conversion from PSI-MI 1.0 to 2.0beta or 2.5 will require a certain amount of remapping and plans to publish details of this will be published on the SourceForge website.

The need for published Minimal Curation Standards was emphasised by the Industry Representatives, who wish to pass these on to third-party vendors of interaction data. A “Minimal Information for Molecular Interaction experiment (MIMI)” document will be written by experienced curators from the public domain databases and made publicly available

The production of a number of tools to assist in the best usage of the PSI-MI XML interchange format was discussed including update of the exsting PSI-maker which converts of tab delineated flat file into PSI-MI format. Yhia tool could also be adapted to convert between Levels 1 and 2, via a flat file intermediate. XML syntactical and semantical validators will also be written to check the validity of the ML files and that CVs have been employed in the correct places. By taking advantage of certain dependencies between CVs (for example Yeast 2-hybrid experiments must always have a bait and prey in the BiologicalRole) rules can be written to check data quality and consistency.

Finally the first draft of the PSI-MI Common Query Interface (PSICQIC) was discussed. This is envisaged as a common interface which will be offered by all participating databases in which a few simple queries will be delivered by query language and a valid PSI_MI document returned. Syntax, error messages etc. should be matched to cPath (BioPax) to make understanding easier for users of both services. An initial specification should be produced within the next 3 months, with a view to further discussion at the HUPO Munich congress.

It is planned to have the updated schema (PSI-MI 2.5) ready for implementation and testing by mid-May 2005 with full release by mid-September.


Mass Spectrometry

The major aims of this workshop session, chaired by Randall Julian (Eli-Lilly) and Weimin Zhu (EBI), were to revise and consolidate the mzData format constructed during the last PSI-MS workshop to work with the supporting controlled vocabularies (CV) and to extend the PSI-MS initiative to the area of molecular identifications using mass spectrometry, mzIdent.

mzData has now been implemented by a number of vendors, for example the standard is now available in Mascot, Phenyx, X! and Bioworks with Proteios citation currently underway. An annual schema release will give sufficient stability for manufacturers whilst still keeping the standard up to date, additional flexibility will be given by a semi-annual CV release. The next minor mzData release will allow an XML namespace and versioning.

The base controlled vocabulary for mzData defines information items which are shared across all experiments and instrument makers All terms include a conserved accession number which is guaranteed to remain constant with the definition until deprecated allowing update of the associated text name without loss of information. The CV has been merged with that of mzXML developed by the ISB and distributed as mzXML.xsd. To ensure the ability to create HUPO submission documents from workflows using such schema, a unified controlled vocabulary was obtained by assigning HUPO accession numbers to all terms included in both the HUPO-PSI schema and the ISB XML schema. When complete, all fixed and variable attributes in mzXML will be mapped to corresponding HUPO-PSI ontology terms and both will be consistent with the IUPAC nomenclature for mass spectrometry. The first version of the mzData CV is currently on SourceForge in spreadsheet format. There will be a further release around August 2005, in OWL format.

The mzIdent standard is being designed to capture results from MS search engines and represent the input parameters for analysis algorithms, thus unifying results from different search engines. The requirements for mzIdent include the need to support the identification of both protein and peptides, by accession number or sequence, and must include the ability to describe modifications. Small molecule identification by either CML or SMILES must also be supported. The possibility of splitting the schema into two, to deal with proteins and peptides separately, was discussed but it was finally decided that adopting one schema in which the molecule type was generalized would allow more flexibility. The file format will now include 3 major elements, that are cross-referenced to each other - a molecular descriptor, an analysis results descriptor and an analysis descriptor.

The molecule descriptor might describe the structural information of a molecule (descriptor, sequence, PTMs, structure), and references to the results. The relationship between molecules, for example peptides as part of proteins will need to be clearly defined, allowing previous results to be used as source evidence. Analysis results might includes scores, spectrum annotation, reference to originating spectra/analysis and to the matched molecule. The analysis descriptor may contain name and version of the search engine and search parameters and can refer to a protocol to define a series of analyses. It also cross-references to the mzData file . A number of use cases for the schema were established, and the need for naming within mzIdent and for the overall structure of the schema to be consistent with that of mzData was emphasised.

A working group which will concentrate on the further development of MzIdent has been established and will aim to report significant progress at the HUPO congress in Munich.


Post-translational modifications

The identification of post-translational modifications, and an understanding of their functional significance is key to all areas of proteomics. The joint session started with presentations by John Garavelli (EBI) and David Creasy (Matrix Science Ltd.) on the RESID and UniMod databases, respectively. The RESID Database of Protein Modifications is a comprehensive collection of annotations and structures for protein modifications and cross-links including pre-, co-, and post-translational modifications, concentrating on naturally-occurring protein modifications. UniMod is a database of protein modifications for use in mass spectrometry applications and contains values for the mass differences introduced by both natural and artificial modifications.

The PSI-MI group has already produced a CV of natural modifications, based on the information in RESID. It was recognized that for use in mass spectrometry such a CV would have to include artificial modifications and a CV that includes both aspects will be produced, for use by all modules within the GPS model.


General Proteomics Standard

The session, chaired by Chris Taylor, started with a review of the current status of the MIAPE documentation. This initiated a debate as to the whether the standards should encompass a “minimum quality of data” suitable for publication. It was felt that decisions on data quality should ultimately be made by the journals and that data capture needs to be complete to enable such decisions to be made. However the possibility of a “MIAPE Light” which would describe a subset of the data, which would be required for deposition in a repository prior to publication may be further discussed with the proteomics journals.

Four MIAPE documents are in various stages of completion

• The 'Parent' Document
• Mass Spectrometry (MS)
• MS Informatics (MSI)
• Gel Electrophoresis (GE)

Following discussion, the GE document was further split into GE and GE Informatics. The version of the MS document which was submitted to the reviewers was circulated and lead to a debate as to whether or not delivery systems should be incorporated into the MS document - MALDI / SELDI are currently integrated into the document but HPLC columns are seen as a separate enough issue to justify an individual document. More specific points included adding a requirement to record the reasonable sensitivity range of the instrument, details of the voltage step-through process for MALDI post-source decay and it was noted that mass accuracy is determined on the day by calibration and this result should be recorded. For some instruments the parameters file may not exist, so there is a need to provide guidance on what to do under these circumstances. Finally, the MS Informatics document was given a preliminary review. The description of experimental controls was clarified and it noted that mzData should be added to the list of peak list formats. Further clarification will be required of the type of spectra that should be included as the raw data may be processed directly. For output, both expected and observed masses of proteins and peptides should be recorded – this will aid in the identification of PTMs although the lack of a CV for artefactual PTMs was observed as an issue.

The group then went on to produce detailed design specifications for Gel-ML, the XML format for capture and interchange of GE data which will form another module within the GPS data model. In analogy to the MS data, two formats will be required:
Gel-ML to capture the process of running a gel, capturing an image associated with a minimal experimental description
GelImAnML (working title) to capture the analysis of an image, to draw in quantitation and also to allow the inclusion of images not related to an actual gel, for example average gels, artificial gels and test images.

Work on the production of PSI-OM, an abstract model to describe a proteomics experiment, was also advance over the course of the workshop. The model will be implementation independent and will be neither hierarchical nor relational. A standard XML schema derived from the model is currently being written and a reference-only database will follow. Both the object model and XML schema are expected to be mature by the Spring 2006 PSI workshop.

The writing of ontologies is key to the entire modelling process and the area where “omics” technologies converge, in that common methodologies can be described by common vocabularies. All-encompassing ontologies need to be usable, accessible and addressable by users. Finally, details of the FuGE object model were described by Angel Pizarro as an example of one potential schema that could be shared by transcriptomics, proteomics and metabolomics; facilitating the generation of data sets containing heterogeneous data from several omics platforms.


Summary

Significant progress was made in all areas over the course of the workshop. The PSI-MI standard has already been widely adopted by both data producers and users but the changes made to the schema during these four days have increased the overall simplicity of the model whilst enabling the exchange of more complex data types, such as complexes and modeled interactions. The introduction of new CVs will make it easier to both parse the data and to filter out unwanted datasets. Additional tools will be provided to aid in conversion between the different PSI-MI levels and to improve data quality. The PSI-MS mzData standard is now being prepared for formal publication but has already been implemented by a number of hardware and software manufacturers, with more expected to follow suit in the near future. The specification for mzIdent has now been fully laid out and it is expected that version 1.0 will be released by the end of 2005. All this work, along with that of the newly formed groups looking at the exchange of gel image and analysis data will be brought together under the umbrella of the GPS, which is now working both on producing a working object model and in the production on ontologies which, in many cases will be shared by many applications and have more wider usage than solely the field of proteomics. One obvious example of this is the CV for protein modification which is to be written for usage across all the GPS modules.
